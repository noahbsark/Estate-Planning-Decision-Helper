The planning landscape

A calmer way to understand the basics

Estate planning can feel complicated, but the first step is usually understanding what each tool is designed to do.

W

A will

A will generally directs what happens to property after death and can name guardians for minor children. It often must pass through probate.

T

A trust

A trust can help manage assets, preserve privacy, avoid probate for funded assets, and plan for incapacity.

B

Many people use both

A trust-based plan often still includes a pour-over will, beneficiary updates, powers of attorney, and health care directives.

§

Complex plans need counsel

Blended families, special-needs beneficiaries, business ownership, taxes, and multi-state property should usually be reviewed by an estate-planning attorney.

1 Clarify family needs

Guardianship, beneficiaries, fiduciaries, and recent life changes.

2 Identify transfer goals

Privacy, probate, real estate, and asset-management preferences.

3 Review complexity

Business ownership, special-needs planning, taxes, or multi-state property.

Questions people ask

Estate-planning FAQ

A few general answers to help you prepare for a more informed conversation.

Is a trust only for wealthy people?

Not necessarily. A trust can be useful for privacy, probate avoidance, incapacity planning, real estate, and smoother asset management. Whether it is worth the cost depends on your goals, assets, family structure, and state law.

Do I still need a will if I have a trust?

Many trust-based estate plans still include a pour-over will. A will can name guardians for minor children and may direct assets that were not transferred into the trust.

Can this tool tell me exactly what I need?

No. This tool is educational and cannot apply your state’s law or provide legal advice. It can help you identify issues to discuss with an estate-planning attorney.

What is probate?

Probate is a court-supervised process for validating a will, appointing someone to administer an estate, paying debts, and distributing property. The timing, cost, and privacy impact vary by state and by situation.

What happens if I have minor children?

A will is commonly used to nominate guardians for minor children. Families may also use trusts to manage money for children until they reach an age or milestone selected in the plan.

When should I update my estate plan?

Consider a review after marriage, divorce, birth or adoption of a child, death of a beneficiary or fiduciary, major asset changes, business changes, moving to another state, or a significant change in tax or estate law.
